Resume Tips

  1. Spellcheck & No typos¬†, Convenient format, Keyword coverage for (in skills/tech section)
    • ACS - Automatic Candidate Screening
    • Include a comprehensive list of skills & tech libraries, but make it easy to read (not comma separated blob of text).
    • Scatter the keywords throughout the resume or include a skills section.
  2. Favor Concise & Brief descriptions. The resume will get maximum 30 seconds of attention before a decision on calling back.
    • Make it so the recruiter gets the full big picture view of your skills in that 30 seconds, not the details. You will go into the details in the phone screen & interview.
  3. Experience, skills, projects and education are most important criteria
    • Order of Emphasis: Technical Work History, Code Projects, then Skills, then Education last
    • Provide easy to find links to example work/projects
  4. Customize your resume towards the job description
    • At first, you‚Äôll have to edit your resume per job (after your 1st Jr Dev job, you can solidify your resume around a single tech stack you like and only apply matching roles

    • So if you haven't built something similar to their product, then it's a very uphill battle.
    • It's one of the fundamental principles of programming, that it's extremely difficult to gauge how much work is hidden behind the statement of a task
    • The "trivial" and the "impossible" can look identical when merely sketched out as an idea in a kanban ticket
    • That's why hiring managers feel much more comfortable if they know you've already done something similar to what they need

Acing the Interview

The Interview vs. The Job

Rehearse

Rehearse

Rehearse

Seriously, rehearse. This early in your career, going into a coding interview and winging it will never get you the results you want.

Obviously, you can't know exactly what will be brought up in an interview, so you will have to improvise a little. But you want to have to improvise as little as possible.

Plan what you are going to say for certain topics that you know will come up. And you can guide the conversation towards those topics to make sure they get covered.

Common Interview Questions Types

  1. Background & Qualifications - 100% chance
    • Prep & rehearse an outline
  2. Behavioral Questions - 75% chance
    • Prep & rehearse your stories
  3. Whiteboarding & Technical Questions - 60% chance
    • If you think you missed a lot of the tech questions, then write them down and use them to study to get better for the next time
    • If you think you only missed one or two and (those might be the deciding factor in their decision) then get the interviewers email and when you get home figure out the right answer and send them the answer in your thank you follow up email in the next 24 hours. They may be impressed in your follow through.
  4. Take home assignments - 40-50% chance
    • Good Harvest Take Home Assessment Solution
      • EagleDream Mockup Example
    • Biobot Analytics Take Home Assessment Solution (FE & BE)
    • Pro-tip: Save a copy of the json response into an example.json file as a backup, so you can still show off the project even if they take down the API endpoint. Just fetch your example.json file instead.

Interview Questions

  1. Strengths Question
    • Find a github project of yours that matches their job description so you can speak about how you already have experience doing what they seek. If you don‚Äôt have a github project that matches yet, spend a week or two and make one before you apply.
  2. Weakness Question
    • Framing it as your learning goals. Mention a tutorial you are already taking to learn the thing in their job description that you lack right now.
    • Frame it as not a weakness but merely being specialized and you will have to lean on a team for other things like design or database knowledge for example.

Time Estimation for Tickets

  • Manager will try to negotiate time (you say it will take 4 weeks, they ask for 2 weeks)
  • First hold your ground. The amount of effort it takes is static, the time criteria they are trying to push on because they are probably feeling pressure from CEO
  • If theykeep pushing, then give them the time they are asking for and renegotiate the effort (i.e. how much stuff to fit into that time)
  • Renegotiating the effort means redefining the scope (i.e. how much stuff to do for this cycle)
  • Break down the tickets into smaller pieces and just fit in the first half of those tickets into the 2 weeks that they want
  • For example, they want authentication (implicitly all the websites) in 2 weeks. Say you can do the authentication for the main dashboard site in 2 weeks. You‚Äôll get to the old, barely visited website next cycle.
